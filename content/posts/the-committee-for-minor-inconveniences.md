There are many conspiracy theories in the world, and I reject nearly all of them on the grounds that they require a level of coordination no group chat has ever achieved. I do, however, believe in one small and specific conspiracy: somewhere, in a windowless municipal building with carpeting the color of oatmeal regret, there is a committee dedicated to creating tiny inconveniences.

Not tragedies. Not catastrophes. Nothing dramatic enough to make the evening news. Their job is more refined than that. They create the sort of problem that makes you pause in a hallway and say, out loud, to nobody, "Really?"

I imagine them meeting every Tuesday at 9:00 a.m. sharp, except Gerald is always late because his badge never scans on the first try, which, to be fair, is considered one of the committee's strongest achievements.

The chairperson, probably named Denise, calls the room to order by tapping a spoon against a ceramic mug that says `World's Best Synergy Driver`. She clears her throat and begins. "Welcome, everyone. Excellent work last week on making fitted sheets impossible to fold in a way that preserves either dignity or geometry. Strong numbers there. Today, we move on to our core mission: making ordinary people suspect the universe has a personal grudge against them."

The interns nod, taking notes in notebooks that never quite lie flat.

The first agenda item is ketchup bottle behavior. A junior analyst presents a slide deck titled `Red Sauce, Blue Mood`. He explains that the bottle should yield nothing for several optimistic squeezes and then, without warning, release enough ketchup to provision a midsized county fair. Someone from Research asks whether the splatter radius can be widened. Denise says yes, but only if it still lands mostly on the food you did not intend to season.

Then comes the elevator subcommittee, a high-performing team if ever there was one. Their philosophy is elegant: the elevator should always appear to have just left, and when it finally arrives it should already contain one man facing the door with the expression of somebody who treats weather as a personal insult. If possible, he will be carrying a box that forces everyone else into an arrangement best described as cooperative geometry.

I know this committee exists because I once pressed the elevator button, waited for a period of time that felt educational, and then decided to take the stairs. At precisely the moment I reached the third-floor landing and regretted all my life choices, I heard the elevator doors open below me with the smug sigh of a machine that had won.

Coincidence? That is what they want you to think.

Their Transportation Division is, if anything, even more ambitious. This is the team responsible for the phenomenon in which the slowest possible walker materializes directly in front of you at the exact moment you are running late, carrying either a violin case, a potted fern, or an attitude of complete spiritual immunity to urgency. They do not shuffle randomly. They move with the serene certainty of a glacier that has accepted tenure.

And if you do manage to overtake them, the committee has already planned Phase Two: your destination will have a door that insists on being pulled when every visual cue, every architectural instinct, and every lesson from childhood says push. There is no apology after this. Only a brief moment of eye contact with the glass, during which you see not your reflection but your limitations.

The committee's work in domestic spaces is especially vicious because it is so intimate. They know your routines. They know how often you misplace your keys. They know that every household contains one drawer whose purpose has quietly expanded from "office supplies" to "museum of cables from extinct civilizations."

That drawer is not an accident. That drawer is policy.

Somewhere, an operations manager stood before a whiteboard and said, "People have too much confidence in their ability to identify charging cords. We need seventeen nearly identical black cables, only two of which matter, and neither should fit the device currently in crisis."

There was applause. One person received a promotion.

I suspect the kitchen team is the committee's most decorated unit. Their portfolio includes the plastic container lid situation, which has now advanced from mild annoyance to abstract art. Every kitchen on Earth contains twenty-three containers and nineteen lids, yet none of them form a legally recognized relationship. You can buy a completely new matching set, place it lovingly into the cupboard, and within forty-eight hours you will discover that the round lid belongs to nothing, the square lid fits emotionally but not physically, and one tiny rectangular container has vanished into a dimension reserved for missing socks and decent pens.

Speaking of pens, the Stationery Task Force deserves scrutiny. They have mastered the science of the disappearing good pen. A bad pen can survive for years at the bottom of a backpack, leaking only when positioned near tax documents. A good pen, however, is like a shy woodland creature. The moment you publicly appreciate it, it evaporates. You lend it to a colleague and it begins a new life under another name.

The committee's greatest innovation, though, is timing. Timing is everything in minor inconvenience design. A toast landing butter-side down is amateur hour. A toast landing butter-side down one minute before guests arrive, after you have just changed your shirt, while your phone rings from another room and the smoke alarm contributes unsolicited harmonies, that is craftsmanship.

This is why I believe the committee recruits from the arts.

No ordinary bureaucrat could engineer the moment when your headphones catch on a doorknob with such emotional precision. That is choreography. That is somebody with a sense of pacing and a dark, playful respect for the human condition. Shakespeare wrote kings. The Committee for Minor Inconveniences writes moments when you reopen the microwave three times because you keep forgetting the spoon.

To be clear, I do not think they are evil. Irritating, yes. Petty, absolutely. But not evil. In a strange way, their work gives shape to modern life. Without them, how would we recognize triumph? If every USB plug went in on the first try, would success still taste as sweet? If every online password reset were simple, would we savor memory as deeply? If every shopping cart rolled straight, what would become of character?

Adversity builds resilience, and apparently some of that adversity is being unable to open a produce bag while standing in a supermarket, rubbing the edges together like a raccoon attempting tax preparation.

I picture the committee members going home after a long day, hanging up their sensible jackets, and accidentally dropping a spoon that somehow catapults yogurt onto a cabinet handle. Perhaps in those moments they, too, pause and stare into the middle distance. Perhaps they whisper, "Outstanding work," with the weary pride of artisans who have become victims of their own success.

Maybe that is the final lesson. Maybe the committee is not a separate group at all. Maybe we are all, in our own small ways, contributing to the grand machinery of inconvenience. Every time we put scissors in the wrong drawer, every time we invent a sign-in flow requiring a verification code from an email that takes four minutes to arrive, every time we wrap an item in packaging that can only be opened with industrial shears kept inside the packaging, we cast a vote.

We become the committee.

This would explain a lot. It would explain why the person who designed the hotel shower controls clearly hated diagrams, why self-checkout machines address innocent fruit like a customs interrogation, and why the printer can detect fear from three rooms away. It would explain why your autocorrect leaves obvious typos untouched but lunges at perfectly reasonable words like an overconfident intern.

It would also mean the only defense is vigilance. We must meet inconvenience with preparation, wisdom, and maybe a backup charger that actually fits something. We must label the mystery cables. We must test the door gently before committing our full body weight. We must never, under any circumstances, say, "This should only take a minute."

Those are famous last words. The committee loves confidence. Confidence is where they do their best work.

So if today your sleeve catches the door handle, your receipt refuses to print, and the website insists your password is wrong despite the fact that you and the password have been through too much together to be having this argument, take heart. You are not cursed. You are simply living in a world maintained by unseen specialists with advanced degrees in nuisance logistics.

And honestly, if they are reading this, I have just one request: leave my fitted sheets out of it. I am not asking to win. I am asking to lose by a narrower margin.
